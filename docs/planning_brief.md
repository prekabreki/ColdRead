# Competitive Landscape for Voice-Over & Script-Formatting Tools: A Feature-Planning Brief for ColdRead

## TL;DR
- **ColdRead occupies a genuine, unfilled niche.** No existing tool ingests a raw script (Markdown/txt/PDF/Word) and outputs a *print-ready, color-coded-by-speaker, wide-leading, breath-group-broken PDF for a human cold read*. The closest paid competitor (Pozotron) marks up scripts for pronunciation/character prep, not first-take reading legibility, and costs $13–16/project plus subscription; the closest free tools (Run Lines Online) are web-only with no PDF export.
- **The strongest defensible differentiation is the "deterministic formatting + AI-only-for-classification" architecture, breath-group segmentation grounded in subtitle line-breaking science, and a persistent pronunciation lexicon in the PLS/IPA/CMU standard** — none of which the incumbents combine.
- **The biggest risks are feature creep** (do NOT build a teleprompter, a DAW, punch-and-roll recording, or a TTS engine — those markets are crowded and orthogonal) **and over-claiming on dyslexia fonts**, where the peer-reviewed evidence is weak-to-negative.

## Key Findings

1. **Screenwriting software solves a different problem.** Final Draft, Celtx, WriterDuet, Arc Studio, Fade In, Highland, Scrivener, Beat, and the Fountain ecosystem are built for *writing and production*, not reading aloud. Their color/revision systems (WGA-standard colored pages, revision asterisks) are about version control, not speaker legibility. None format for breath groups or cold-read legibility.
2. **Teleprompters are a large, mature, adjacent market** with voice-tracking (PromptSmart, Teleprompter.com), open-source options (Textream, several GitHub projects), and subscription pricing $8–20/mo. They solve *scrolling while reading to camera*, not *preparing a printable script*. This is complementary to ColdRead, not competitive.
3. **Pozotron is the closest true competitor** but is a downstream audiobook proofing + prep SaaS, priced per-finished-hour, aimed at audiobook narrators/studios, and not open source. Its script-prep markup targets pronunciation and character consistency, not cold-read formatting.
4. **Pronunciation research is a real, time-consuming pain point** with an established toolchain (Forvo, YouGlish, Merriam-Webster, howjsay) and an established standard (W3C PLS + SSML phoneme tags, IPA/CMU alphabets) already adopted by Amazon Polly, Azure, and ElevenLabs. ColdRead can adopt this standard cheaply and be interoperable.
5. **Breath-group segmentation has a strong evidence base** in subtitle line-breaking (Netflix/BBC "break at natural linguistic boundaries") and reading-prosody research — but **dyslexia fonts and Bionic Reading do not**. ColdRead should lean on the former and hedge on the latter.
6. **Timing estimation is well-standardized** (roughly 150–160 WPM audiobook, 160–180 commercial, 140–160 e-learning) and cheap to implement; existing calculators already do it.
7. **The cold-read use case specifically is underserved.** Voice actors describe marking up scripts by hand, often with minutes of prep, and there is no dedicated tool that automates that markup into a legible printable artifact.

## Details

### 1. Screenwriting & script-formatting software

**Final Draft** (industry standard, ~$199.99 one-time digital download, two activations, Windows/macOS/iOS; upgrades not included and cost ~$79.99) offers the deepest production feature set: a **Character Highlighter** (highlight one character's dialogue), **Revision Mode** with the WGA/Cole & Haag standard colored-page cycle (White → Blue → Pink → Yellow → Green → Goldenrod → Buff → …), revision asterisks in the margin, cast reports, scene reports, and the ability to embed page colors into exported/printed PDFs. Critically, its color system is about *revision tracking*, not *who is speaking* — the opposite of ColdRead's speaker color-coding.

**Celtx** (subscription, from ~$14.99/mo "Writer" tier; cloud, all devices) is a full pre-production pipeline (breakdowns, scheduling, budgeting, call sheets). **WriterDuet** (free for 3 scripts, paid ~$5.99–9.99/mo billed annually) is collaboration-focused. **Arc Studio** (free tier + $99/year Pro) is a clean modern editor that, as of research, still lacked colored pages/starred revisions ("coming soon"). **Fade In** is a well-regarded lower-cost one-time-purchase Final Draft alternative with Fountain support.

**The Fountain ecosystem** is the most relevant open-source analog. Fountain is a free/open-source plain-text screenplay markup (like Markdown for screenplays), created by John August and Stu Maschwitz. It is explicitly *not* designed for production features: the Fountain site states it excludes "MOREs, CONTINUEDs, revision marks, locked pages, or colored pages." Open-source/free tools include **Trelby** (GPL, Windows/Linux, PDF export, revision tracking, character navigation), **Afterwriting** (web-based, customizable PDF preview, FDX↔Fountain conversion), **Beat** (free/open-source, macOS/iOS, Fountain-native, PDF/FDX export), **fountain-mode** (Emacs, accurate pagination), and **Better Fountain** (VS Code). Ian Thomas's open-source **screenplay-tools** library parses Fountain and FDX in JavaScript, Python, C#, and C++ — potentially useful for ColdRead if it ever wants to ingest screenplay formats.

**Implication for ColdRead:** the screenwriting world already has robust, often-free formatting and PDF export, but *all of it targets standard screenplay layout*. None optimizes for a human reading aloud on first sight. ColdRead should support importing Fountain/FDX (via existing open libraries) but should not try to become a screenwriting editor.

### 2. Teleprompter / prompter software

This is a crowded, mostly-subscription market. **PromptSmart** pioneered **VoiceTrack** (speech-recognition scrolling that holds your place if you go off-script); pricing is ~$9.99/mo or $99.99/yr, with a confusing three-app ecosystem (Lite/Pro/+) and a separate "Extended" add-on (~$1.99/mo) for text inversion, narrow margins, and Selfie Mode. Users on the App Store have complained bitterly about voice-tracking regressions after a 2.0 update ("whatever the developer changed that has screwed up voice tracking has made the app pretty much useless"). **Teleprompter.com** advertises adaptive voice recognition with ~120ms latency and cross-device sync, from ~$9.99/mo. **BIGVU**, **SpeakFlow**, **VEED**, and others cluster at $7.50–20/mo (annual), typically watermarking free tiers. One-time-purchase options (e.g., ShareSpeak, ~$12.50 lifetime) exist but are rarer.

The features that matter here — scroll-speed control, voice-tracking, mirror mode, remote/foot-pedal control, current-line highlighting, countdown/elapsed timers, dark mode, multi-device sync — are well covered.

**Open-source teleprompters are plentiful:** Eyevinn's web teleprompter (controller/display separation, mirror mode, dark theme, duration monitor), JHenriquesss/OpenTeleprompter (Rust/Tauri, offline, 0.25×–10× speed, mirror, countdown, rehearsal mode with word count + reading-time estimate, resume position), **Textream** (free macOS, word-tracking highlight + voice-activated scroll), jlecomte/voice-activated-teleprompter (web, Vosk-based), antrys/daves-prompter (Vosk, cross-platform, remote/mirroring), and CueMode (VS Code extension with line-height, mirror, focus mode).

**Implication for ColdRead:** A teleprompter is a *reading-delivery* tool; ColdRead is a *script-preparation* tool. They are complementary. ColdRead should NOT build a teleprompter — the space is saturated including in open source — but could add value by *exporting to teleprompter-friendly formats* (clean plain text with breath breaks, or a large-type continuous mode).

### 3. Voice-over & audiobook production tools

**Pozotron** is the single most important competitor to understand. It is a cloud-based, AI-powered audiobook **proofing + script-prep** suite. Its Script Prep Tools comprise three parts: a **Pronunciation Guide** (scans the manuscript, extracts complex/rare/foreign words, one-click lookups to Oxford GB/US, Merriam-Webster, Forvo, and YouGlish, plus custom recorded pronunciation snippets shareable with authors), a **Character Voice Guide** (scans for character names, stores voice samples, notes, accent cues, image reminders for series consistency), and **Automated Script Markups** (exports a ready-for-narration manuscript with script highlighting and phonetic pronunciation comments). Its proofing tool uses AI to catch misreads, inserted/missed words, and long pauses, and exports DAW marker files, pickup packets, and subtitle files.

Pozotron pricing (2026) is billed **per finished hour (PFH)** with subscription tiers, and it no longer publishes a static price grid (it uses a "Find my Plan" calculator + demo-gated trial). Per Pozotron's own support docs: **Pay-As-You-Go** = no monthly fee, $15/PFH proofing + $16/project for Script Prep; **Narrator Plan** = $99/mo with 16 hours included, ~$9/additional hour, single narrator; a higher **Professional** tier around $119/mo (16 hrs included, ~$10.35/PFH overage, $14/project Script Prep); **Publisher** tiers (call for pricing) for studios. A free trial exists but requires booking a demo first, then grants a one-month trial + 7 free proofing hours. Script Prep can be bought standalone ($16/project) without a proofing subscription.

**Crucially, Pozotron's markup is for pronunciation/character prep and downstream QC — not cold-read reading legibility.** It does not produce ColdRead's color-coded-by-speaker, wide-leading, breath-group PDF for first-take reading. It is also not open source, is cloud-only (a privacy consideration for unpublished manuscripts), and is priced for working audiobook professionals, not casual or one-off users.

**Recording/DAW tools** (Descript, Adobe Audition, Twisted Wave, Reaper, Audacity, Audiate) own the **punch-and-roll** workflow — record over a mistake with a few seconds of pre-roll to match tone/cadence — which is the standard for long-form audiobook recording. Descript's 2026 plans run Free / Hobbyist $16/mo (annual) / Creator $24/mo / Business $50/mo. **Speechify Studio** (TTS/AI-voice generation) runs Free / Starter ~$19/user/mo / Creator ~$49/user/mo. **Descript** and **Audiate** offer transcription-driven editing. These are *recording/editing* tools, orthogonal to ColdRead's *pre-recording prep* niche. Notably, opinion is divided on punch-and-roll's necessity (some coaches call it essential; others, like Gabrielle Nistico and Lance Blair, argue it's oversold), so ColdRead should not assume a punch-and-roll integration is table stakes.

**Dubbing/ADR** is served by **VoiceQ** (KIWA; macOS + cloud, ~900 languages, digital rythmo band / "karaoke" scrolling, Pro Tools/Nuendo/Logic/Reaper integration, script manager, speech-detection cueing; monthly/annual/perpetual licenses) and **Synchro Arts** (VocALign/Revoice Pro for audio alignment). Rythmo band gives 35–50 lines/hour vs 10–12 for traditional ADR. This is a specialized, high-end niche and not ColdRead's target — but the rythmo-band concept (annotating breaths, mouth openings, syllable length on a scrolling guide) validates the demand for breath-level annotation.

**What narrators say they need in script prep** (from Pozotron's blog, NarratorsRoadmap, VoiceOverXtra, and VO coaches): pre-read the whole manuscript; build a pronunciation list early (prioritizing repeated words like a fantasy kingdom's name); catch accent/character requirements *before* recording (the classic cautionary tale: discovering near the end that a main character should have had an accent); and mark up the script as a "roadmap." Prep time is often tight — voice actors describe being handed sides an hour before an audition or being expected to "get in the booth and go" on a 50-page training module — which is exactly ColdRead's wedge.

### 4. Pronunciation & lexicon tools

Professional narrators handle pronunciation through a well-known toolchain: **Forvo** (crowdsourced native-speaker audio; ~456K–613K monthly visits), **YouGlish** (pronunciations in real YouTube context), **Merriam-Webster**, **howjsay**, **Hearnames**, VOA's pronunciation guide, and specialist resources like **AudioEloquence** (maintained by narrators Judith West and Heather Henderson). Narrators typically build a spreadsheet of tricky words during a pre-read, cross-check multiple sources, and sometimes phone locals for place names.

**There is an established standard worth adopting:** the **W3C Pronunciation Lexicon Specification (PLS) 1.0** — an XML format grouping `<lexeme>` entries (grapheme + phoneme), referenced from SSML via `<lexicon>`, using IPA or vendor alphabets. **SSML `<phoneme>` tags** provide inline, per-instance pronunciation. This stack is already implemented by **Amazon Polly**, **Microsoft Azure** (IPA + x-microsoft-sapi), and **ElevenLabs** (PLS `.pls` files, IPA + CMU Arpabet; note phoneme tags only work on specific models like eleven_flash_v2/v3, otherwise use `alias` substitution). PLS is case-sensitive, and EPUB 3 adopted a namespaced SSML phoneme/alphabet attribute (though uptake was limited due to weak reading-system support).

**Implication for ColdRead:** its "pronunciation hints for tricky proper nouns" feature is a real differentiator, and it should (a) let users export/import a **persistent pronunciation lexicon in PLS/IPA (and optionally CMU)** so the lexicon is reusable across scripts and interoperable with TTS platforms, and (b) offer one-click links to Forvo/YouGlish per flagged word (as Pozotron does). A human-readable respelling hint (not just IPA) matters, because narrators reading a printout benefit more from "nuclear → NOO-klee-er" than from `/ˈnjuːkliər/`.

### 5. Accessibility & dyslexia-friendly reading formatting

**The evidence splits sharply.** For **line-breaking at natural linguistic boundaries**, the evidence and professional consensus are strong. Netflix's English (USA) Timed Text Style Guide caps lines at 42 characters (16 for CJK), max two lines, and ~17 characters/second reading speed for adult content, and requires breaks at "logical linguistic units." Its verbatim guidance is to break lines "at natural grammatical boundaries — after commas, before conjunctions, or between subject and verb... Never break in the middle of a proper noun or between an article and its noun," and to "prefer a bottom-heavy pyramid shape for subtitles when multiple line break options present themselves, but avoid having just one or two words on the top line." The **BBC Subtitle Guidelines** similarly require breaks "at natural points." Reading-prosody research (phrasing, "breath groups," the prosody pyramid of thought-group → focus word → stressed syllable) and classroom fluency practice (slash-marking phrase boundaries: single slash = short pause, double = sentence end) directly validate ColdRead's breath-group segmentation. An arXiv TTS-expressivity paper explicitly frames "Breath Group organization" as "a well-formed group of words conveying a concept," analogous to a line of poetry — exactly ColdRead's model.

**For dyslexia fonts and Bionic Reading, the peer-reviewed evidence is weak-to-negative.** Rello & Baeza-Yates, "Good Fonts for Dyslexia" (ACM SIGACCESS ASSETS 2013, DOI 10.1145/2513383.2513447), found OpenDyslexic "did not enhance text readability or reading speed" and that participants "preferred Verdana and Helvetica to OpenDyslexic" — with a larger effect coming from font size and spacing than from typeface. Wery & Diliberto (*Annals of Dyslexia* 2016/17, 67(2):114–127), testing 12 dyslexic students, found "no improvement in reading rate or accuracy." Kuster et al. (2018, DOI 10.1007/s11881-017-0154-6) concluded "the Dyslexie font neither benefits nor impedes the reading process of children with and without dyslexia." A W3C literacy expert similarly noted that "research doesn't really have a lot of evidence showing that these special fonts help people read faster or make fewer mistakes." Bionic Reading has been challenged by controlled studies finding no benefit (and sometimes slower reading). A dissenting 2019 eye-tracking abstract (Franzen et al.) found OpenDyslexic changed visual reading strategy and aided comprehension in adults — so it is not unanimous. **Lexend** has somewhat better provenance (developed at Vanderbilt using reading-fluency data). The British Dyslexia Association's own guidance favors clean sans-serifs (Arial, Verdana), generous spacing, and good formatting — which help *everyone*.

**Implication for ColdRead:** the defensible, evidence-backed accessibility features are **generous leading/spacing, large type, short line length, and breath-group/clause-based line breaks** — not the font gimmicks. ColdRead can *offer* OpenDyslexic/Lexend as user options but should **not market them as proven**; framing them as "some readers prefer" is honest and avoids overclaiming. Note that ColdRead currently renders in Courier (monospace) for grayscale-print robustness; a proportional option (or Lexend/Atkinson Hyperlegible) could improve legibility, and Atkinson Hyperlegible (from the Braille Institute) is a stronger evidence-adjacent choice than OpenDyslexic.

### 6. Timing estimation

Words-per-minute standards are well-established and consistent across sources: **audiobook narration ~150–160 WPM**. The most-cited figure is **155 WPM / 9,300 words per finished hour, which is the ACX/Audible standard** — per audiobook narrator and industry writer Karen Commins, "Audible uses an average rate of speed of 155 words a minute, or 9300 words per finished hour," a benchmark also used by Penguin Random House Audio and Macmillan Audio. Other genre norms: **conversational/narration ~130–150 WPM**, **commercials ~160–180 WPM**, **e-learning ~140–160 WPM**, **IVR/telephony ~110 WPM**, and slower (110–130) for dramatic/emotional literary reads. Existing calculators (VOWordCounter's Script Timer, audiobook length calculators) already convert word count → runtime with genre presets, and correctly warn that raw word count misleads because prices, phone numbers, and URLs "expand when spoken."

**Implication for ColdRead:** a per-archetype runtime estimate is cheap to add, genuinely useful (helps a reader know if a :30 commercial script will overrun its slot), and differentiates from screenwriting page-count timing (which assumes ~1 page/minute of screen time, irrelevant to VO). ColdRead should estimate spoken length per archetype and flag scripts likely to overrun a target duration, ideally accounting for number/URL expansion.

### 7. Gaps & opportunities

**What is NOT well served:**
- **A dedicated cold-read formatting artifact.** Screenwriting tools format for production; teleprompters format for scrolling; Pozotron formats for pronunciation/QC. Nobody produces a *printable, speaker-color-coded, breath-group-broken, wide-leading PDF for a human reading aloud on first sight*. This is ColdRead's white space. (The adjacent Run Lines Online offers a free web "Audition Side Formatter" and "Monologue Breath Mark Generator" that approximate parts of the idea, but feed an AI scene-partner reader and produce no print-ready PDF.)
- **Free/open-source + local/offline prep.** Pozotron is cloud-only and paid; ColdRead's local-first, MIT-licensed, no-account model is a real differentiator for privacy-sensitive (embargoed manuscripts, NDA'd corporate scripts) and cost-sensitive users. (Note the PyMuPDF AGPL dependency caveat for redistributors.)
- **Multi-format ingestion into a reading-optimized output.** Most tools ingest one or two formats; ColdRead's Markdown/txt/PDF/Word intake feeding a single clean PDF is unusually flexible.
- **Deterministic, "AI-classifies-but-never-rewrites" trust model.** In an era of narrator anxiety about AI replacing them and about AI silently altering text, ColdRead's guarantee ("same script + toggles → same PDF; your words come out exactly as written") is a marketing and trust asset the competition can't easily claim.

**Where existing tools already win (don't rebuild):** revision/version control (Final Draft), recording and punch-and-roll (DAWs), voice-tracked scrolling (teleprompters), AI proofing/pickup detection (Pozotron), TTS generation (ElevenLabs/Speechify), and dubbing sync (VoiceQ). ColdRead should interoperate with, not clone, these.

## Recommendations

**Build now (core differentiation):**
1. **Double down on breath-group segmentation quality**, explicitly grounded in Netflix/BBC line-breaking rules and prosody research: break at clauses and before conjunctions, and — per Netflix's verbatim rule — never break in the middle of a proper noun or between an article and its noun. This is your most defensible, evidence-backed feature. Make the "density" of breaks adjustable (sparse/natural/dense), mirroring how Run Lines Online's breath-mark generator works.
2. **Ship a persistent, standards-based pronunciation lexicon.** Store user pronunciations across sessions/scripts; export/import as **W3C PLS (IPA, optionally CMU)** for interoperability with Polly/Azure/ElevenLabs; but *display* human-readable respellings on the printed PDF. Add one-click Forvo/YouGlish lookups per flagged word.
3. **Add per-archetype runtime estimation** with standard WPM presets (audiobook 155, commercial 170, e-learning 150, narration 140), and flag likely overruns, accounting for number/URL/acronym expansion.
4. **Keep and market the deterministic, local-first, "never rewrites your text" architecture** as a trust guarantee. This is a genuine wedge against cloud AI tools.

**Build soon (high value, low cost):**
5. **Fountain/FDX import** via existing open-source parsers (screenplay-tools) to capture the screenwriting-adjacent audience.
6. **A large-type "continuous reading" / teleprompter-friendly plain-text export** so ColdRead output can feed open-source teleprompters (Textream, Eyevinn) — interoperate rather than compete.
7. **Offer Lexend and Atkinson Hyperlegible as optional proportional fonts** alongside Courier, framed honestly ("some readers find these easier"), without evidence-overclaiming.

**Do NOT build (avoid feature creep):**
- A teleprompter engine (saturated, incl. open source).
- A DAW / punch-and-roll recorder (owned by Audition, Reaper, Audacity, Descript; and its necessity is contested).
- A TTS/AI-voice generator (ElevenLabs/Speechify/Polly own this; also politically fraught with your VO-artist audience).
- AI proofing/pickup detection (Pozotron's moat; expensive to match).
- Dubbing/rythmo-band sync (VoiceQ's niche).
- **Do not market dyslexia fonts as clinically proven** — the evidence doesn't support it and it risks credibility.

**Benchmarks that would change these recommendations:**
- If users overwhelmingly request recording integration, consider a *lightweight export to DAW markers* (as Pozotron does) rather than a full recorder.
- If a well-funded competitor (e.g., Pozotron) ships a free cold-read PDF formatter, accelerate the pronunciation-lexicon and local-first privacy advantages, which are harder for a cloud SaaS to match.
- If Fountain/screenplay import drives adoption, prioritize deeper screenplay-format fidelity.

## Caveats
- **Pozotron's exact 2026 pricing is partially uncertain:** it no longer publishes a static price grid, and several tier figures (Narrator $99, Professional $119, overage $8.20–10.35/PFH) come from support docs that may be slightly dated. Treat these as approximate.
- **Speechify Studio and Descript figures come from third-party pricing trackers**, not verified on the vendors' live pages during this research; Descript's Hobbyist/Creator/Business structure showed strong cross-source consensus, Speechify Studio's less so.
- **The dyslexia-font evidence is genuinely mixed**, not uniformly negative; a minority of studies show benefits. The safe position is "reduces subjective effort for some readers," not "improves reading performance."
- **"No tool does exactly what ColdRead does" is a strong claim** based on thorough but not exhaustive search; niche or newly launched tools may exist. The adjacent categories are well-mapped, and the specific combination (raw-file → color-coded-by-speaker, breath-group, wide-leading print PDF, free/open-source, local) was not found in any single product.
- Several sources are vendor blogs or marketing pages (Celtx, Pozotron, teleprompter comparison sites with affiliate incentives); where possible, claims were cross-checked against primary standards (W3C, Netflix Partner Help Center) and peer-reviewed research (ACM ASSETS, Annals of Dyslexia).
